Functional Analysis: A Didactic Tutorial

Chapter 1: Real Analysis — A Quick Review

This chapter reviews foundational concepts in real analysis which underpin the more abstract machinery of functional analysis. Even readers with strong mathematical backgrounds will benefit from a concise, structured refresher.

1.1 Limits and Continuity

Definition (Limit): Let \( f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \). We say that \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \) if for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \), there exists \( \delta > 0 \) such that whenever \( 0 < |x - a| < \delta \), we have \( |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon \).

Continuity is defined as the property that the function value and the limit coincide:

Definition (Continuity): A function \( f \) is continuous at \( x = a \) if \( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) \).

Proof and Worked Example

Example: Let \( f(x) = 3x^2 - 5 \). Show that \( \lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 7 \) using the epsilon-delta definition.
Proof: We want to show that for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \), there exists \( \delta > 0 \) such that: \[ |x - 2| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - 7| < \varepsilon. \] Note that: \[ f(x) = 3x^2 - 5 \Rightarrow f(x) - 7 = 3x^2 - 12 = 3(x^2 - 4) = 3(x - 2)(x + 2). \] So, \[ |f(x) - 7| = 3|x - 2||x + 2|. \] To bound this, restrict \( \delta \leq 1 \) so that \( |x - 2| < 1 \Rightarrow 1 < x < 3 \Rightarrow |x + 2| < 5 \). Then: \[ |f(x) - 7| < 3|x - 2| \cdot 5 = 15|x - 2|. \] Thus, to ensure \( |f(x) - 7| < \varepsilon \), it suffices to choose \( \delta = \min\left(1, \frac{\varepsilon}{15}\right) \).

Exercises

1. Let \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \). Is \( f \) continuous at \( x = 0 \)? Justify using the epsilon-delta definition.
2. Prove that \( f(x) = x^2 \) is continuous everywhere on \( \mathbb{R} \) using the epsilon-delta definition.
3. Consider the piecewise function: \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & \text{if } x < 1 \\\\ 2 & \text{if } x = 1 \\\\ 2x - 1 & \text{if } x > 1 \end{cases} \] Is \( f \) continuous at \( x = 1 \)? Prove or disprove.

1.2 Differentiation and Integration

Differentiation measures local linear change. Integration aggregates values over intervals and extends to Lebesgue integration in more advanced contexts.

Example: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus states that if \( f \) is continuous on \( [a,b] \), and \( F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt \), then \( F'(x) = f(x) \).

Proof and Worked Example

Example: Let \( f(x) = x^2 \). Show that its derivative at a point \( a \in \mathbb{R} \) is \( f'(a) = 2a \).
Proof: Using the definition of the derivative, \[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(a + h)^2 - a^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2ah + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} (2a + h) = 2a. \]
Example: Evaluate \( \int_0^1 (3x^2 - 2x + 1)\,dx \).
Solution: Use basic antiderivatives: \[ \int_0^1 (3x^2 - 2x + 1)\,dx = \left[ x^3 - x^2 + x \right]_0^1 = (1 - 1 + 1) - 0 = 1. \]

Exercises

1. Let \( f(x) = \sqrt{x} \). Use the definition of the derivative to find \( f'(x) \) for \( x > 0 \).
2. Prove that if \( f \) is differentiable at \( a \), then it is continuous at \( a \).
3. Compute \( \int_1^2 \frac{1}{x}\,dx \) and interpret the result geometrically.
4. State and prove the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part I.

1.3 Metric Spaces

Definition (Metric Space): A metric space is a pair \( (X, d) \) where \( X \) is a set and \( d: X \times X \to [0, \infty) \) is a function satisfying:
  • \( d(x, y) = 0 \Leftrightarrow x = y \)
  • \( d(x, y) = d(y, x) \)
  • \( d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z) \) (Triangle Inequality)

Proof and Worked Example

Example: Show that the function \( d(x, y) = |x - y| \) defines a metric on \( \mathbb{R} \).
Proof: To verify that \( d(x, y) = |x - y| \) is a metric, check the three axioms:
  1. Non-negativity and identity: \( |x - y| \geq 0 \), and \( |x - y| = 0 \iff x = y \).
  2. Symmetry: \( |x - y| = |y - x| \).
  3. Triangle inequality: For all \( x, y, z \in \mathbb{R} \): \[ |x - z| \leq |x - y| + |y - z|. \] This follows from the standard triangle inequality for absolute value.
Hence, \( d \) is a metric.
Example: Consider \( d(x, y) = (x - y)^2 \). Is this a metric?
Answer: No. Although non-negative and symmetric, it fails the triangle inequality. Consider: \[ d(0, 1) + d(1, 2) = 1^2 + 1^2 = 2,\quad d(0, 2) = 4. \] Since \( 2 < 4 \), the triangle inequality fails.

Exercises

1. Prove that \( d(x, y) = \sqrt{(x_1 - y_1)^2 + (x_2 - y_2)^2} \) defines a metric on \( \mathbb{R}^2 \).
2. Give an example of a set with a metric where not all triangles are isosceles or equilateral.
3. Let \( d(x, y) = \min(1, |x - y|) \) on \( \mathbb{R} \). Prove this is a valid metric. What topology does it induce?

1.4 Completeness

Definition (Complete Metric Space): A metric space \( (X, d) \) is complete if every Cauchy sequence in \( X \) converges to a limit in \( X \).
Example: \( \mathbb{R} \) with the usual metric \( d(x,y) = |x - y| \) is a complete metric space.

Proof and Worked Example

Example: Prove that \( \mathbb{R} \), with the usual metric \( d(x, y) = |x - y| \), is a complete metric space.
Proof: Let \( (x_n) \) be a Cauchy sequence in \( \mathbb{R} \). Then for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \), there exists \( N \in \mathbb{N} \) such that for all \( m, n \geq N \), \( |x_n - x_m| < \varepsilon \). Since \( \mathbb{R} \) is a complete ordered field, every Cauchy sequence converges to a limit \( x \in \mathbb{R} \). Therefore, \( \mathbb{R} \) is complete.
Example: Consider the metric space \( (0, 1) \) with the standard metric. Is it complete?
Answer: No. The sequence \( x_n = 1 - \frac{1}{n} \) is Cauchy in \( (0, 1) \), but it converges to 1, which is not in the open interval. Thus, \( (0, 1) \) is not complete.

Exercises

1. Prove that every convergent sequence is Cauchy. Is the converse always true?
2. Show that the set of rational numbers \( \mathbb{Q} \) is not complete by constructing a Cauchy sequence that does not converge in \( \mathbb{Q} \).
3. Let \( c_0 \) be the set of all real sequences converging to 0, with the sup norm. Prove that \( c_0 \) is a complete metric space.

1.5 Sequences and Series of Functions

We distinguish between pointwise and uniform convergence:

Definition (Uniform Convergence): A sequence \( \{f_n\} \) converges uniformly to \( f \) on \( D \) if: \[ \forall \varepsilon > 0, \exists N \text{ such that } n \geq N \Rightarrow \sup_{x \in D} |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon. \]

Proof and Worked Example

Example: Let \( f_n(x) = \frac{x}{n} \) on \( [0,1] \). Show that \( f_n \to 0 \) uniformly.
Proof: For each \( x \in [0,1] \), \[ |f_n(x) - 0| = \left| \frac{x}{n} \right| \leq \frac{1}{n}. \] Hence, \[ \sup_{x \in [0,1]} |f_n(x) - 0| \leq \frac{1}{n} \to 0, \] so \( f_n \to 0 \) uniformly on \( [0,1] \).
Example: Let \( f_n(x) = x^n \) on \( [0,1] \). Show that \( f_n \to f \), where \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } 0 \leq x < 1 \\\\ 1 & \text{if } x = 1 \end{cases} \] and determine if the convergence is uniform.
Answer: For \( x \in [0,1) \), \( x^n \to 0 \). At \( x = 1 \), \( x^n = 1 \). The pointwise limit is the function \( f \) defined above. However, the convergence is not uniform: \[ \sup_{x \in [0,1)} |x^n - 0| = \sup_{x \in [0,1)} x^n = 1 \text{ for all } n. \] Hence, the supremum does not go to zero, so the convergence is not uniform.

Exercises

1. Give an example of a sequence of functions that converges pointwise but not uniformly.
2. Let \( f_n(x) = \frac{\sin(nx)}{n} \). Show that \( f_n \to 0 \) uniformly on \( \mathbb{R} \), but that the series \( \sum f_n \) does not converge uniformly.
3. Prove that if \( f_n \to f \) uniformly and each \( f_n \) is continuous, then \( f \) is continuous.

Chapter 2: Normed Vector Spaces

This chapter introduces normed vector spaces — the building blocks of functional analysis. A norm assigns a notion of "length" or "size" to vectors in a vector space, generalizing the concept of Euclidean length.

2.1 Definitions and Properties

Definition (Norm): A norm on a vector space \( V \) is a function \( \|\cdot\| : V \to [0, \infty) \) such that for all \( x, y \in V \), and all scalars \( \alpha \):
  • \( \|x\| = 0 \Leftrightarrow x = 0 \)
  • \( \|\alpha x\| = |\alpha| \cdot \|x\| \)
  • \( \|x + y\| \leq \|x\| + \|y\| \) (Triangle Inequality)

2.2 Examples of Norms

Example (Euclidean Norm): In \( \mathbb{R}^n \), the norm \( \|x\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2} \) defines the standard Euclidean norm.
Example (\( p \)-Norms): For \( 1 \leq p < \infty \), define: \[ \|x\|_p = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|^p\right)^{1/p} \] and for \( p = \infty \): \[ \|x\|_\infty = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} |x_i| \]

2.3 Topological Structure

The norm induces a metric on \( V \) via \( d(x, y) = \|x - y\| \), turning \( V \) into a metric space. This allows us to talk about convergence, continuity, open and closed sets, and compactness.

2.4 Bounded Linear Operators

Definition (Bounded Operator): Let \( X, Y \) be normed spaces. A linear operator \( T : X \to Y \) is bounded if there exists \( C > 0 \) such that: \[ \|Tx\|_Y \leq C \|x\|_X \quad \text{for all } x \in X. \]

The set of all bounded linear operators from \( X \) to \( Y \), denoted \( \mathcal{B}(X, Y) \), forms a normed vector space with the operator norm:

\[ \|T\| = \sup_{\|x\| \leq 1} \|Tx\| \]

2.5 Equivalent Norms

Two norms \( \|\cdot\|_1 \) and \( \|\cdot\|_2 \) on a vector space \( V \) are equivalent if they induce the same topology, i.e., there exist constants \( c, C > 0 \) such that:

\[ c\|x\|_1 \leq \|x\|_2 \leq C\|x\|_1 \quad \text{for all } x \in V. \]

In finite dimensions, all norms are equivalent — a powerful simplification not available in infinite-dimensional spaces.

Chapter 3: Banach Spaces

A Banach space is a normed vector space that is complete with respect to the metric induced by its norm. Completeness ensures the existence of limits for all Cauchy sequences, making Banach spaces fundamental to analysis.

3.1 Definition and Basic Properties

Definition (Banach Space): A normed vector space \( (X, \|\cdot\|) \) is called a Banach space if every Cauchy sequence in \( X \) converges to an element in \( X \).
Example: \( \mathbb{R}^n \), with any norm, is a Banach space because \( \mathbb{R}^n \) is complete.
Example: The space \( \ell^p \) (sequences \( x = (x_n) \) such that \( \sum |x_n|^p < \infty \)) is a Banach space for \( 1 \leq p \leq \infty \).

3.2 Key Theorems

Theorem (Uniform Boundedness Principle): Let \( X \) be a Banach space and \( \{T_\alpha\} \subset \mathcal{B}(X, Y) \). If \( \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| < \infty \) for all \( x \in X \), then \( \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| < \infty \).
Theorem (Hahn-Banach): If \( p: V \to \mathbb{R} \) is a sublinear function and \( f: U \to \mathbb{R} \) is a linear functional dominated by \( p \) on a subspace \( U \subset V \), then \( f \) can be extended to the whole space without increasing its norm.
Theorem (Open Mapping Theorem): If \( T: X \to Y \) is a surjective bounded linear operator between Banach spaces, then \( T \) maps open sets to open sets.
Theorem (Closed Graph Theorem): If \( T: X \to Y \) is a linear operator between Banach spaces, and its graph is closed in \( X \times Y \), then \( T \) is bounded.

3.3 Dual Spaces

The dual space \( X^* \) of a normed space \( X \) consists of all bounded linear functionals on \( X \). It is always a Banach space, even if \( X \) is not.

Example: If \( X = \ell^p \), then \( X^* = \ell^q \), where \( 1/p + 1/q = 1 \), for \( 1 < p < \infty \).

3.4 Reflexivity

Definition (Reflexive Space): A Banach space \( X \) is reflexive if the natural map from \( X \) into its double dual \( X^{**} \) is surjective.

Reflexivity has implications for compactness and weak convergence. Spaces like \( \ell^p \) for \( 1 < p < \infty \) are reflexive; \( \ell^1 \) and \( \ell^\infty \) are not.

Chapter 4: Inner-Product Spaces and Hilbert Spaces

This chapter introduces inner-product spaces, which allow for the definition of geometric concepts like angles and orthogonality. A Hilbert space is a complete inner-product space and serves as the foundation for many areas in mathematics and physics.

4.1 Inner-Product Spaces

Definition (Inner Product): An inner product on a vector space \( V \) over \( \mathbb{R} \) or \( \mathbb{C} \) is a function \( \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : V \times V \to \mathbb{F} \) satisfying:
  • \( \langle x, x \rangle \geq 0 \) and \( \langle x, x \rangle = 0 \iff x = 0 \)
  • \( \langle x, y \rangle = \overline{\langle y, x \rangle} \)
  • \( \langle ax + by, z \rangle = a\langle x, z \rangle + b\langle y, z \rangle \)
Example: In \( \mathbb{R}^n \), the standard inner product is \( \langle x, y \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i \).

The norm induced by an inner product is \( \|x\| = \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle} \).

4.2 Hilbert Spaces

Definition (Hilbert Space): An inner-product space \( H \) is called a Hilbert space if it is complete with respect to the norm induced by its inner product.
Example: The space \( \ell^2 \), consisting of square-summable sequences \( x = (x_n) \) with \( \sum |x_n|^2 < \infty \), is a Hilbert space.

4.3 Orthogonality and Projections

Two vectors \( x, y \) are orthogonal if \( \langle x, y \rangle = 0 \). The concept of orthogonal projection is central in Hilbert space theory.

Theorem (Projection Theorem): Let \( H \) be a Hilbert space and \( M \subset H \) a closed subspace. Then for any \( x \in H \), there exists a unique \( m \in M \) such that \( \|x - m\| = \inf_{y \in M} \|x - y\| \). Moreover, \( x - m \) is orthogonal to \( M \).

4.4 Orthonormal Bases

Definition (Orthonormal Set): A set \( \{e_i\} \subset H \) is orthonormal if \( \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \delta_{ij} \).

Every separable Hilbert space has a countable orthonormal basis \( \{e_n\} \), and any element \( x \in H \) can be expressed as:

\[ x = \sum_n \langle x, e_n \rangle e_n \]

4.5 Riesz Representation Theorem

Theorem (Riesz Representation): Let \( H \) be a Hilbert space. For every bounded linear functional \( f \in H^* \), there exists a unique \( y \in H \) such that \( f(x) = \langle x, y \rangle \) for all \( x \in H \).

Chapter 5: The “Great Theorems” of Linear Functional Analysis in Normed Vector Spaces

This chapter presents the foundational theorems that underpin much of modern functional analysis. These results guarantee the structure and behavior of linear maps between normed and Banach spaces, providing critical tools for analysis and PDE theory.

5.1 Hahn–Banach Theorem

Theorem (Hahn–Banach): Let \( p: V \to \mathbb{R} \) be a sublinear function and \( f: U \to \mathbb{R} \) a linear functional on a subspace \( U \subset V \) such that \( f(x) \leq p(x) \) for all \( x \in U \). Then \( f \) can be extended to a linear functional \( F: V \to \mathbb{R} \) such that \( F(x) \leq p(x) \) for all \( x \in V \).

This result allows bounded linear functionals defined on subspaces to be extended to the whole space, preserving boundedness.

5.2 Uniform Boundedness Principle

Theorem (Uniform Boundedness Principle): Let \( X \) be a Banach space and \( \{T_\alpha\} \subset \mathcal{B}(X, Y) \) a family of bounded linear operators. If for every \( x \in X \), the set \( \{\|T_\alpha x\|\} \) is bounded, then \( \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| < \infty \).

This principle ensures pointwise boundedness implies uniform boundedness on bounded sets in Banach spaces.

5.3 Open Mapping Theorem

Theorem (Open Mapping): Let \( T: X \to Y \) be a bounded linear operator between Banach spaces and assume \( T \) is surjective. Then \( T \) maps open subsets of \( X \) onto open subsets of \( Y \).

This guarantees the continuity of inverse operators when they exist and are defined on the whole space.

5.4 Closed Graph Theorem

Theorem (Closed Graph): Let \( T: X \to Y \) be a linear operator between Banach spaces. If the graph of \( T \), defined by \( \{(x, Tx) \mid x \in X\} \), is closed in \( X \times Y \), then \( T \) is bounded.

This result is useful when the operator cannot be explicitly bounded, but one can show that its graph is closed.

5.5 Application: Inverse Mapping

Corollary (Inverse Mapping Theorem): If \( T: X \to Y \) is a bijective bounded linear operator between Banach spaces, then \( T^{-1}: Y \to X \) is also bounded.

This corollary follows from the Open Mapping Theorem and is foundational for operator theory and solving linear PDEs.

Chapter 6: Locally Convex Spaces and Distributions

Locally convex spaces generalize normed spaces and are fundamental to modern analysis, particularly in the study of distributions and topological vector spaces.

6.1 Locally Convex Spaces

Definition (Locally Convex Space): A topological vector space \( X \) is locally convex if its topology can be generated by a family of seminorms \( \{p_i\}_{i \in I} \).

This allows for a flexible framework in which convergence and continuity can be studied even without a norm.

Example: The space of continuous functions \( C([a,b]) \), with the family of seminorms \( p_K(f) = \sup_{x \in K} |f(x)| \) over compact subsets \( K \subset [a,b] \), is locally convex.

6.2 Seminorms

Definition (Seminorm): A seminorm on a vector space \( V \) is a function \( p: V \to \mathbb{R} \) such that:
  • \( p(x + y) \leq p(x) + p(y) \)
  • \( p(\alpha x) = |\alpha| p(x) \)

Unlike a norm, \( p(x) = 0 \) does not imply \( x = 0 \).

6.3 Distributions

Distributions extend the concept of functions and derivatives. They allow one to rigorously define solutions to differential equations that involve discontinuities or singularities.

Definition (Distribution): A distribution is a continuous linear functional on the space \( \mathcal{D}(\mathbb{R}^n) \) of compactly supported smooth functions (test functions).
Example: The Dirac delta \( \delta \) is a distribution defined by \( \delta(\phi) = \phi(0) \) for all test functions \( \phi \).

6.4 Weak Derivatives

In the distributional sense, a function \( f \) has a weak derivative \( f' \) if:

\[ \int f(x) \phi'(x) dx = -\int f'(x) \phi(x) dx \quad \text{for all } \phi \in \mathcal{D}(\mathbb{R}) \]

This definition makes it possible to differentiate functions that are not classically differentiable, such as step functions.

Chapter 7: Fourier Transform and Calderón–Zygmund Singular Integral Operators

This chapter explores the Fourier transform as a powerful tool for analyzing functions and distributions, and introduces Calderón–Zygmund theory as a framework for singular integral operators, central in modern harmonic analysis and PDEs.

7.1 The Fourier Transform

Definition (Fourier Transform): For \( f \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n) \), the Fourier transform is defined as: \[ \hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(x) e^{-2\pi i x \cdot \xi} \, dx \]

The Fourier transform extends to \( L^2(\mathbb{R}^n) \) as a unitary operator and to tempered distributions \( \mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^n) \).

7.2 Properties of the Fourier Transform

  • Linearity: \( \widehat{af + bg} = a\hat{f} + b\hat{g} \)
  • Translation: \( \widehat{f(x - y)}(\xi) = e^{-2\pi i y \cdot \xi} \hat{f}(\xi) \)
  • Differentiation: \( \widehat{\partial_j f}(\xi) = 2\pi i \xi_j \hat{f}(\xi) \)
  • Convolution: \( \widehat{f * g} = \hat{f} \cdot \hat{g} \)

7.3 Tempered Distributions and Schwartz Space

The Schwartz space \( \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n) \) consists of smooth, rapidly decaying functions. Its dual \( \mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^n) \) contains tempered distributions, which support generalized Fourier transforms.

Example: The Fourier transform of the Dirac delta \( \delta \) is the constant function 1.

7.4 Calderón–Zygmund Operators

Definition (Singular Integral Operator): A linear operator \( T \) is a Calderón–Zygmund singular integral operator if it is bounded on \( L^2(\mathbb{R}^n) \), and its kernel \( K(x, y) \) satisfies:
  • \( |K(x, y)| \leq \frac{C}{|x - y|^n} \)
  • \( |K(x, y) - K(x', y)| + |K(y, x) - K(y, x')| \leq C \frac{|x - x'|}{|x - y|^{n+1}} \)

Such operators are foundational in harmonic analysis and appear in the study of singular integrals, PDEs, and boundary value problems.

Chapter 8: Applications to Linear Partial Differential Equations

This chapter discusses how the tools of functional analysis apply to the study of linear partial differential equations (PDEs), especially through the lens of weak formulations, existence and uniqueness results, and the use of Sobolev spaces.

8.1 Weak Formulation of PDEs

In functional analysis, PDEs are often reformulated in a weak (distributional) sense, allowing us to study solutions that may not be classically differentiable.

Example: The weak formulation of the Poisson equation \( -\Delta u = f \) in \( \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n \) becomes: \[ \int_\Omega \nabla u \cdot \nabla v \, dx = \int_\Omega f v \, dx \quad \text{for all } v \in H_0^1(\Omega) \]

8.2 Sobolev Spaces

Definition (Sobolev Space): For \( k \in \mathbb{N} \) and \( 1 \leq p \leq \infty \), the Sobolev space \( W^{k,p}(\Omega) \) consists of functions \( u \in L^p(\Omega) \) whose weak derivatives up to order \( k \) also lie in \( L^p(\Omega) \).

Hilbertian Sobolev spaces \( H^k(\Omega) := W^{k,2}(\Omega) \) are particularly useful due to their inner product structure.

8.3 Lax–Milgram Theorem

Theorem (Lax–Milgram): Let \( H \) be a Hilbert space, \( a: H \times H \to \mathbb{R} \) a continuous coercive bilinear form, and \( f \in H^* \). Then there exists a unique \( u \in H \) such that: \[ a(u, v) = f(v) \quad \text{for all } v \in H \]

This theorem ensures existence and uniqueness of weak solutions to many linear PDEs.

8.4 Green’s Functions and Fundamental Solutions

Green’s functions represent the influence of point sources and are crucial in solving linear PDEs explicitly in certain domains.

8.5 Elliptic Regularity

Under suitable conditions, weak solutions to elliptic PDEs are in fact smooth. This is a cornerstone of modern PDE theory, linking weak solutions to classical solutions.

Chapter 9: Differential Calculus in Normed Vector Spaces

This chapter introduces the extension of differential calculus to normed vector spaces, providing the foundation for analyzing smooth maps in infinite-dimensional settings.

9.1 Fréchet Derivative

Definition (Fréchet Derivative): Let \( X \) and \( Y \) be normed vector spaces, and \( U \subset X \) open. A function \( f: U \to Y \) is Fréchet differentiable at \( x \in U \) if there exists a bounded linear map \( A: X \to Y \) such that: \[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\|f(x + h) - f(x) - A(h)\|_Y}{\|h\|_X} = 0. \] The map \( A \) is called the Fréchet derivative of \( f \) at \( x \), denoted \( Df(x) \).

9.2 Gâteaux Derivative

Definition (Gâteaux Derivative): The Gâteaux derivative of \( f: U \to Y \) at \( x \in U \) in the direction \( h \in X \) is: \[ D_G f(x; h) = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(x + t h) - f(x)}{t}, \] if the limit exists.

Unlike the Fréchet derivative, the Gâteaux derivative is directional and may not be linear or continuous in \( h \).

9.3 Relationship Between Derivatives

If \( f \) is Fréchet differentiable at \( x \), then it is also Gâteaux differentiable at \( x \), and the derivatives coincide. The converse is generally false without additional conditions (e.g., continuity or uniform boundedness).

9.4 Chain Rule and Higher Derivatives

If \( f: U \to Y \) and \( g: V \to Z \) are Fréchet differentiable and \( f(U) \subset V \), then \( g \circ f \) is Fréchet differentiable and: \[ D(g \circ f)(x) = Dg(f(x)) \circ Df(x). \]

Higher derivatives can be defined inductively: \( D^2 f(x) = D(Df)(x) \), resulting in multilinear maps representing Hessians in finite or infinite-dimensional settings.

9.5 Taylor Expansion

If \( f \) is \( n \)-times Fréchet differentiable at \( x \), we have a Taylor expansion: \[ f(x + h) = f(x) + Df(x)h + \frac{1}{2!}D^2f(x)(h,h) + \dots + \frac{1}{n!}D^n f(x)(h,\dots,h) + o(\|h\|^n). \]

Chapter 10: Differential Geometry in \( \mathbb{R}^n \)

This chapter introduces the geometric structures of smooth manifolds and submanifolds embedded in Euclidean spaces. It focuses on tangent spaces, vector fields, and the differential geometry of surfaces.

10.1 Tangent Vectors and Spaces

Definition (Tangent Vector): At a point \( p \in \mathbb{R}^n \), a tangent vector can be represented as a directional derivative operator acting on smooth functions.

The tangent space \( T_p \mathbb{R}^n \) is isomorphic to \( \mathbb{R}^n \) itself.

10.2 Submanifolds

Definition (Submanifold): A subset \( M \subset \mathbb{R}^n \) is a \( k \)-dimensional submanifold if for every point \( p \in M \), there exists a neighborhood \( U \subset \mathbb{R}^n \) and a smooth map \( \phi: U \to \mathbb{R}^{n-k} \) such that: \[ M \cap U = \{ x \in U : \phi(x) = 0 \}, \quad D\phi \text{ has full rank}. \]

10.3 Vector Fields and Flows

A vector field assigns a tangent vector to each point of a manifold. Flows generated by vector fields describe dynamical systems and evolution over time.

10.4 The Differential and Pushforward

Definition (Differential): Let \( f: M \to N \) be a smooth map between manifolds. The differential \( df_p: T_pM \to T_{f(p)}N \) is a linear map defined by: \[ df_p(v)(g) = v(g \circ f), \quad \text{for all } g \in C^\infty(N). \]

10.5 Curvature and the Second Fundamental Form

The second fundamental form measures how a surface bends in \( \mathbb{R}^n \). It encodes extrinsic curvature and leads to geometric quantities like mean and Gaussian curvature.

Example: For a surface \( S \subset \mathbb{R}^3 \), the Gaussian curvature at a point is the product of the principal curvatures, and the mean curvature is their average.

Chapter 11: Calculus of Variations

This chapter develops the fundamental framework of the calculus of variations, which seeks to find functions that minimize or extremize functionals—integrals depending on unknown functions and their derivatives.

11.1 Functionals and Extremals

Definition (Functional): A functional assigns a real number to a function. The typical form is: \[ J[y] = \int_a^b L(x, y(x), y'(x)) \, dx \] where \( y \) lies in a suitable function space.

The goal is to find functions \( y \) such that \( J[y] \) is minimized or maximized.

11.2 Euler–Lagrange Equation

Theorem (Euler–Lagrange): If \( y \) is a minimizer of \( J[y] \), then it satisfies: \[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{\partial L}{\partial y'} \right) = 0 \] under appropriate smoothness and boundary conditions.

11.3 Natural Boundary Conditions

When variations are not fixed at the endpoints, additional terms in the variation lead to boundary conditions that must be satisfied by extremals.

11.4 Higher-Order and Multiple Variables

The Euler–Lagrange formalism extends to higher derivatives and multiple dependent variables. For a second-order Lagrangian:

\[ J[y] = \int_a^b L(x, y, y', y'') \, dx \Rightarrow \text{a more complex Euler–Lagrange equation} \]

11.5 Applications

  • Geodesics: Curves minimizing distance in a Riemannian manifold.
  • Physics: Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics derive from extremal principles.
  • Elasticity: Minimization of strain energy in deformable bodies.

Chapter 12: “Great Theorems” of Nonlinear Functional Analysis

This chapter presents foundational theorems in nonlinear functional analysis, particularly those built on topological methods such as Brouwer's and Schauder's fixed point theorems and Leray–Schauder degree theory.

12.1 Brouwer’s Fixed Point Theorem

Theorem (Brouwer): Every continuous map \( f: B^n \to B^n \) from the closed unit ball in \( \mathbb{R}^n \) to itself has at least one fixed point.

This theorem is fundamental in finite dimensions and forms the basis for generalizations in infinite-dimensional spaces.

12.2 Schauder Fixed Point Theorem

Theorem (Schauder): Let \( C \) be a closed convex subset of a Banach space and \( T: C \to C \) a continuous compact operator. Then \( T \) has at least one fixed point.

This extends Brouwer's theorem to infinite dimensions and is widely applicable to nonlinear PDEs.

12.3 Leray–Schauder Degree

The Leray–Schauder degree generalizes the topological degree from finite dimensions to compact perturbations of the identity in Banach spaces. It provides existence results for operator equations \( T(x) = x \).

12.4 Applications

  • Existence of solutions: Used in nonlinear boundary value problems and differential inclusions.
  • Bifurcation theory: Analyzing solution branches of nonlinear equations.
  • Nonlinear eigenvalue problems: Generalizing spectral theory to nonlinear maps.
